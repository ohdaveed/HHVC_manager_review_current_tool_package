A mold problem is always an excess moisture problem. This moisture generally comes from two sources: leaks or condensation.

## 1. Leaks (Landlord's Responsibility)

This includes things like a dripping pipe, a water heater leak, or a leak in the roof or window frame. These are the landlord's responsibility to fix.

**What you should do:** Please notify your landlord in writing as soon as you discover any leak. Mold can start to grow in just a couple of days, so reporting it quickly is the best way to prevent a small leak from becoming a big problem.

## 2. Condensation (Tenant's Responsibility)

This is moisture from everyday living—like cooking, showering, and even breathing—that builds up in the air. When this damp, warm air hits a cold surface (like a window, an exterior wall, or a cold door), it turns back into liquid water. This is the "sweat" you see on windows. This type of moisture is generally the tenant's responsibility to manage.

## SFDPH / Environmental Health action threshold

As a reminder, the San Francisco Department of Public Health (SFDPH) can act when mold is growing on the walls and ceiling, and the affected area totals at least 10 square feet. The owner is then required to clean the mold and paint with mold resistant paint. Please note that we cannot assist with mold found on personal belongings like furniture, clothing, or other household items.

## Tips to Control Moisture and Prevent Mold

Here are some tips to help you control indoor moisture from condensation:

1. Monitor your indoor humidity: You can buy a device called a hygrometer (often available online for around $15) to measure the moisture in your air. Try to keep the humidity level below 55% (ideally between 40-50%).
2. Air Out Your Home Daily: Open windows for at least 5-15 minutes each day to "flush" out the damp, warm air and let in drier, fresh air.
3. Use Exhaust Fans When Cooking & Showering: Run the exhaust fan in your kitchen or bathroom. Leave it on for another 15-20 minutes after you finish clearing out all the steam.
   - Pro Tip: You can check if your fan is working by seeing if the suction will hold up a single square of toilet tissue.
4. Keep the Air Moving: Run a table fan, floor fan, or pedestal fan when you're home. This constant air movement is one of the most effective ways to stop damp, stagnant air from settling on walls and in corners.
5. Reduce Steam: When cooking, try to cover your pots and pans with a lid. Avoid drying large amounts of laundry on indoor racks.
6. Give Furniture Room to Breathe: Pull your furniture and other large items at least 6 inches away from cold, exterior walls. This prevents damp, stagnant air from getting trapped.
7. Wipe Away Moisture: If you see condensation on your windows or sills, wipe it up with a towel. This stops the water from dripping down and soaking into the wall.
8. Clean Mold Correctly: For small areas of mold, the best way to clean it is with simple soap and water. We don't recommend using bleach.
9. Let Your Closets Breathe: Avoid overstuffing closets, especially if they are on an exterior wall. Try to leave the doors slightly open to allow for air flow.
10. Wash Moldy Items: If you have mold on clothes or other fabrics, you can usually just wash them in warm water and dry them completely.
11. Filter Your Air: Consider using an air purifier with a HEPA filter. This won't remove the cause of the mold, but it can help capture the microscopic mold spores floating in the air.

## Why Does Good Airflow Help So Much?

You'll notice many of the tips involve fans and open windows. Airflow is your best tool against mold for three main reasons:

1. It removes damp air: The most important job of ventilation is to flush out the humid air from cooking and showering and replace it with drier air from outside. This new air is a "powerful drying force".
2. It prevents "sweat" (condensation): Air movement from a fan mixes the air in the room, which helps warm up cold surfaces (like windows and exterior walls). When these surfaces aren't as cold, moisture is less likely to turn into water droplets on them.
3. It dries surfaces faster: If "sweat" does form, a fan helps evaporate it quickly. Mold needs water to sit for a while to start growing, and airflow dries things up before that can happen.
