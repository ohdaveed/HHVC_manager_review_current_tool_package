# Learn what happens after you report a housing or pest problem

Learn how reports are reviewed, when an inspector may contact you, and what to expect during a Healthy Housing and Vector Control inspection.

## This page can help if you are:
- A tenant or resident who has reported a pest, mold, garbage, or building problem to 311
- A property owner or manager wanting to understand the inspection and enforcement process
- A building worker or SRO resident waiting for an inspection follow-up

---

## 1. How a report moves through the City
When you report a housing or pest problem, your report follows a standard public health service pathway:
1. **Intake and Routing**: Your report is submitted through San Francisco 311 and is automatically routed to the Healthy Housing and Vector Control (HHVC) program.
2. **Public Health Review**: HHVC reviews the complaint to see if it involves a housing, sanitation, or pest-related public health concern under San Francisco Health Code Article 11.
3. **Agency Hand-off (If applicable)**: If your complaint involves structural water-intrusion (such as leaking roof frames, structural plumbing leaks, or broken water heaters), HHVC will route your concern to our sister agency, the Department of Building Inspection (DBI). HHVC focuses strictly on condensation, humidity, and ventilation-related moisture issues.

---

## 2. Inspector contact expectations
How and when an inspector contacts you depends on the details you provided in your report:
* **If you provided contact information**: An HHVC inspector may contact you directly to ask follow-up questions, clarify the location of the problem, or schedule a physical inspection visit.
* **If you reported anonymously**: An inspection may still occur without notice when appropriate. This typically applies to problems that can be verified from shared areas, communal SRO spaces, building exteriors, or yards.
* **Property Owner Notification**: If you are a tenant, HHVC expects you to notify your property owner or manager in writing first and allow them at least 72 hours to respond before requesting a City inspection. An inspector will verify whether this notice was given.

---

## 3. What to expect during an inspection
HHVC inspectors conduct inspections to verify health code violations and ensure safe living environments:
1. **The Site Visit**: An inspector will visit the property to review the reported problem. In SRO residential hotels, the inspection may cover private rooms as well as shared areas (such as communal kitchens, communal bathrooms, hallways, or garbage rooms).
2. **Code Evaluation**: The inspector evaluates the conditions against Health Code standards. For example, they look for active rodent breeding, bed bug infestations, or visible mold on walls or ceilings. HHVC can enforce corrections on visible mold only if the affected area totals at least 10 square feet on structural surfaces.
3. **Notice of Violation**: If the inspector finds violations, they will issue a formal Notice of Violation (NOV) to the property owner or manager. This notice outlines the specific conditions that must be corrected.
4. **Compliance and Reinspection**: The property owner is given a specific compliance period to make repairs or hire professionals (such as a licensed Pest Control Operator). The inspector will conduct a follow-up reinspection to verify the conditions have been corrected. If the owner fails to correct the conditions, reinspection fees, citations, or a Director's Hearing may apply.

---

## 4. What this page does not promise
To set realistic public expectations, please note that HHVC cannot guarantee the following:
* **No exact contact or inspection times**: HHVC cannot promise an exact time or date when an inspector will call you or arrive at your building.
* **No pre-determined outcomes**: We cannot promise a specific inspection finding or guarantee that a Notice of Violation will be issued. 
* **No jurisdiction over personal belongings**: HHVC enforces corrections only on building structures (walls, ceilings, ventilation systems). We do not have legal authority over personal property such as furniture, clothes, or household belongings.
* **No immediate resolution**: Structural repairs and professional pest treatments take time. Abatement must be verified professionally (for example, bed bug abatement requires a 28-day monitoring period of zero pest activity before a violation is cleared).
