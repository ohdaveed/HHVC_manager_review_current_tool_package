## 3. Why Prevention and Airflow Matter
A mold problem is always an excess moisture problem. Daily human living—such as cooking, showering, washing clothes, and even breathing—releases substantial amounts of moisture (steam and water vapor) into the air. 

When this damp, warm air encounters cold surfaces (such as windows, cold doors, or exterior walls), it cools rapidly and turns back into liquid water. This is commonly referred to as "sweat" or condensation. Constant moisture on these cold surfaces provides the ideal breeding ground for mold.

### Airflow is your best defense against mold for three physical reasons:
1. **It removes damp air:** Good ventilation actively flushes out moisture-laden indoor air and replaces it with drier, fresh air from the outside, acting as a powerful drying force.
2. **It prevents "sweat" (condensation):** Moving air from a fan circulates and mixes the air in the room, which helps warm up cold surfaces like windows and exterior walls. When these surfaces aren't as cold, moisture is far less likely to condense into liquid water.
3. **It dries surfaces faster:** If condensation does form, airflow from a fan accelerates evaporation. Mold requires standing water to sit on a surface for an extended period to germinate; airflow dries the water up before mold can establish itself.

## 4. Practical Steps to Control Moisture
Under Karl CMS component guidelines, critical prevention steps are kept fully visible on the page rather than hidden inside collapsible accordions.

### 1. Monitor your indoor humidity
* **Action:** Invest in a hygrometer (often available online for around $15) to measure the relative humidity of the air inside your home.
* **Goal:** Keep your indoor humidity level below 55%, with an ideal range between 40% and 50%. High humidity creates a perfect environment for mold to thrive.

### 2. Air out your home daily
* **Action:** Open your windows for at least 5 to 15 minutes each day.
* **Goal:** This simple habit "flushes" damp, stagnant indoor air out and lets in drier, fresh outdoor air, preventing humidity buildup.

### 3. Use exhaust fans when cooking and showering
* **Action:** Always run the exhaust fan in your kitchen while cooking and in your bathroom when showering or bathing.
* **Goal:** Leave the fans running for an additional 15 to 20 minutes *after* you finish to ensure all residual steam and moisture are completely cleared from the space.
* **Pro Tip:** You can easily check if your exhaust fan's suction is working properly by holding a single square of toilet tissue against the fan's grate. If the suction holds the tissue in place, the fan is operating correctly.

### 4. Reduce steam production
* **Action:** Cover pots and pans with lids while cooking to prevent water vapor from releasing into the air. 
* **Action:** Avoid drying large amounts of wet laundry on indoor racks, which releases substantial moisture. Use an outdoor line or a vented clothes dryer. If indoor drying is unavoidable, ensure you open a window or run an exhaust fan nearby.

### 5. Give furniture room to breathe
* **Action:** Pull furniture, wardrobes, bookshelves, and large storage items at least 6 inches away from cold, exterior walls.
* **Goal:** This physical gap allows continuous air circulation and prevents stagnant, damp air from getting trapped in dark spaces behind furniture, which is a prime location for hidden mold.

### 6. Wipe away condensation immediately
* **Action:** If you see condensation or "sweat" forming on windows, metal frames, sills, or cold doors, wipe it up promptly with a clean, dry towel.
* **Goal:** Preventing water from dripping down stops moisture from soaking into drywall or wood sills and breeding mold.

### 7. Optimize closet airflow
* **Action:** Avoid overstuffing closets, especially those located on cold exterior walls.
* **Action:** Keep closet doors slightly open whenever possible to allow for continuous air circulation and dissipate humidity.

### 8. Clean small mold areas correctly
* **Action:** For small patches of mold, scrub the area gently using a simple solution of soap and water, then dry the surface thoroughly.
* **Warning on Bleach:** Do not use bleach to clean mold. Bleach is corrosive, produces harsh fumes, and does not penetrate porous materials—leaving hidden mold roots alive beneath the surface.
* **Warning on Painting:** Never paint over mold. Painting over mold is a temporary cosmetic cover that does not address the moisture problem. Mold will continue to grow beneath the paint layer, causing structural damage.

### 9. Wash moldy fabrics promptly
* **Action:** If you find mold on clothes, linens, or other washable fabrics, wash them in a standard warm water cycle and dry them completely.
* **Goal:** Ensure items are 100% dry before storing them to prevent mold from re-establishing.

### 10. Utilize air filtration as an ancillary measure
* **Action:** Consider using an air purifier equipped with a True HEPA filter.
* **Goal:** While a HEPA filter cannot fix the underlying moisture source, it actively captures floating microscopic mold spores from the air, improving overall indoor air quality and reducing allergens.

## 5. When to Report Instead (Tenant vs. Landlord Roles)
Responsibility for mold and moisture is split under San Francisco health and housing rules depending on the physical source of the water.

### Landlord Responsibilities (Structural Leaks)
Property owners and managers are responsible for maintaining a building's structure and systems. This includes fixing:
* Dripping or leaking pipes (plumbing failures)
* Malfunctioning or leaking water heaters
* Roof leaks (water penetrating ceilings or attics)
* Window frame leaks (failing exterior seals)
* Foundation or ground-seepage leaks

**What you must do:** If you rent your home, notify your landlord or property manager of any structural leak in writing immediately. Given that mold can germinate within 24 to 48 hours, prompt reporting prevents severe damage.

### Tenant Responsibilities (Condensation and Ventilation Management)
Tenants are responsible for managing moisture generated by daily living activities by utilizing fans, opening windows, keeping closets clear, and wiping up condensation as detailed in the practical steps.

### When to Contact 311 for a City Inspection
1. **The 72-Hour Notice Window:** If you have notified your landlord in writing about an active leak or ventilation failure (such as an inoperable exhaust fan), you must allow them **at least 72 hours** to respond or start making repairs.
2. **Escalation:** If the landlord does not respond within 72 hours, refuses to make repairs, or if there is an immediate health or safety concern, report the problem to 311 to request an inspection.
3. **SFDPH Inspection Thresholds:** 
   * Healthy Housing and Vector Control (HHVC) has legal authority to inspect and enforce corrective actions when visible mold is actively growing on structural surfaces (walls or ceilings) and the affected area totals **at least 10 square feet**.
   * **No Personal Property Jurisdiction:** SFDPH does not have legal jurisdiction over personal belongings. HHVC cannot inspect or issue enforcement actions for mold found on furniture, clothes, bedding, or other personal household items. It is the resident's responsibility to clean or discard personal items.
