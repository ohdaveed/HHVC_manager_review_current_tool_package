# Reduce indoor moisture, condensation, and humidity

Learn how to reduce moisture, condensation, and humidity that can lead to mold.

## Who this page is for

This page can help if you are:

- A renter
- An SRO or residential hotel resident
- A property owner
- A property manager
- A building maintenance worker

## Why prevention and airflow matter

A mold problem is always an excess moisture problem [77, 78]. Daily living activities like showering, cooking, and even breathing release water vapor into the air [77, 79, 90]. When this warm, damp air hits a cold surface—like a window, an exterior wall, or a door—it cools rapidly and turns back into liquid water, which is the "sweat" or condensation you see [77, 79, 90].

Good airflow is your best defense against mold because it works in three ways [89, 90]:

1. **It removes damp air:** Ventilation actively flushes out moisture-laden indoor air and replaces it with drier air from the outside, acting as a powerful drying force [89, 90].
2. **It prevents "sweat" (condensation):** Moving air circulates and mixes the air, which helps warm up cold surfaces. When surfaces aren't as cold, condensation cannot form [89, 90].
3. **It dries surfaces faster:** If sweat does form, airflow evaporates it quickly. Mold needs moisture to sit on a surface for an extended period to begin growing; airflow dries things before mold can establish itself [89, 90].

## Practical steps to control moisture

You can manage indoor condensation and prevent mold by following these 9 strategies [81]:

### 1. Monitor your indoor humidity

Measure your air moisture using a device called a **hygrometer** [81, 82]. Try to keep the relative humidity level below 55% (ideally between 40% and 50%) [81, 82]. High humidity creates a conducive environment for mold to thrive [82].

### 2. Air out your home daily

Open your windows for 5 to 15 minutes every day [81, 83]. This simple routine "flushes" damp, stagnant air out of your home and brings in drier, fresh outdoor air [81, 83].

### 3. Use exhaust fans when cooking and washing

Always run the exhaust fan in your kitchen while cooking and in your bathroom when showering [81, 83]. To make sure all steam and moisture are completely cleared, leave the fans running for 15 to 20 minutes after you finish [81, 83].

- **Pro Tip:** To check if your exhaust fan is working correctly, hold a single square of toilet tissue against the fan grate. If the fan's suction holds the paper in place, it is operating with enough force [81, 83].

### 4. Reduce steam and moisture production

Cover your pots and pans with lids when cooking to block steam from escaping into the room [81, 84]. Avoid drying large amounts of wet laundry on indoor drying racks, as this releases massive amounts of humidity [81, 84]. If you must dry clothes indoors, open a nearby window or run a fan to ensure adequate ventilation [84].

### 5. Give your furniture room to breathe

Pull wardrobes, bookshelves, sofas, and other large furniture at least 6 inches away from cold exterior walls [81, 85]. This gap allows air to circulate freely and stops damp air from getting trapped in stagnant pockets behind furniture [81, 85].

### 6. Wipe away condensation immediately

If you see condensation or "sweat" pooling on windows, frames, sills, or other cold surfaces, wipe it up promptly with a clean, dry towel [81, 85]. This prevents water from dripping down, soaking into your walls, and creating a breeding ground for mold [81, 85].

### 7. Let your closets breathe

Do not overstuff your closets, especially those located on cold exterior walls [81, 87]. When possible, leave closet doors slightly open to allow continuous airflow and dissipate trapped moisture [81, 87].

### 8. Clean small areas of mold correctly

For small, isolated patches of mold, clean them using a simple solution of soap and water [81, 86]. Gently scrub the area, rinse, and dry it completely [86].

- **Important Warnings:**
  - **Do not use bleach:** Bleach is not recommended for mold removal. It is corrosive, harmful to surfaces, and often fails to penetrate porous materials, leaving the roots of the mold intact [86].
  - **Never paint over mold:** Painting over mold is a temporary cover-up that does not address the underlying moisture issue. Mold will continue to grow beneath the paint, causing more extensive damage and health risks. You must completely remove the mold and dry the surface before repainting [81, 86].

### 9. Wash moldy fabrics and filter your air

If you find mold on clothes, bedding, or other washable fabrics, wash them in warm water and dry them completely before storing [81, 87].

- **HEPA Air Filters:** You may also consider using an air purifier equipped with a HEPA filter [81, 88]. While HEPA filters are helpful ancillary tools to capture floating mold spores and improve air quality, they do not fix the moisture source itself [81, 88].

## When to report instead

If your mold problem is caused by a structural defect or if your landlord has not addressed a leak, you may need to file a formal complaint [77].

### Understanding Responsibilities

- **Leaks are the Landlord's Responsibility:** This includes dripping pipes (under sinks or inside walls), water heater leaks, roof leaks, foundation seepage, and broken window frames [77, 79]. Landlords must repair these structural or systemic failures [77, 79].
- **Condensation is the Tenant's Responsibility:** General humidity and surface "sweat" caused by daily living must be managed by the resident using proper ventilation practices [77, 79].

### What renters should do first

If you discover a leak or a moisture problem that requires structural repair, notify your landlord or property manager **in writing** immediately [77, 79]. Mold can begin growing in as little as 24 to 48 hours, so quick reporting is essential [79]. Under city guidelines, you should give your property owner or manager at least **72 hours** to respond or begin fixing the problem [96].

### Reporting to SFDPH (311)

If the landlord fails to act after 72 hours, or if the moisture problem continues, you can report the issue through **311** [96].
Please note the strict guidelines for city inspection and enforcement:

1.  **Strict 10-Square-Foot Threshold:** The San Francisco Department of Public Health (SFDPH) can only take enforcement action when mold is actively growing on structural surfaces (such as walls and ceilings) and the affected area totals **at least 10 square feet** [78, 80].
2.  **No Jurisdiction Over Personal Items:** SFDPH does not have the authority to inspect, clean, or replace moldy personal belongings, such as furniture, clothing, or other household items [78, 80]. These remain the tenant's responsibility [80].
