# PREFACE

These Director’s Rules and Regulations explain the required practices for preventing and controlling rodents and other vectors in San Francisco. The rules support Article 11 of the San Francisco Health Code. They apply to homes, businesses, yards, vacant lots, and other built environments.

The goal of this document is to protect public health by reducing the presence of animals and insects that can cause disease, contaminate food or living spaces, or damage property. These rules also help promote safe, healthy, and well‑maintained housing.

The San Francisco Department of Public Health (DPH) encourages cooperation among property owners, managers, tenants, businesses, and public agencies. Everyone shares responsibility for preventing pest infestations.

DPH supports the use of Integrated Pest Management (IPM), which uses the least‑toxic methods possible. This document provides guidance to help owners and tenants understand their responsibilities. It does not replace the role or professional judgment of licensed pest control operators.

# I. INTRODUCTION

These Rules and Regulations describe best practices for controlling and preventing pests. They are based on Article 11 of the San Francisco Health Code and apply citywide.

**Vectors** are animals or insects that can spread disease or cause harm. Examples include: • Rodents

• Fleas

• Mosquitoes

• Wasps

• Pigeons

• Raccoons

• Mice

• Rats

DPH works to reduce vector‑borne diseases using safe and effective methods. Successful control depends on teamwork among: • Property owners and managers

• Tenants

• Community groups

• Businesses

• Pest control professionals

• Other city agencies

Key parts of vector prevention include: • Keeping buildings and outdoor areas clean

• Blocking pests from entering buildings

• Detecting infestations early

• Coordinating efforts among all responsible parties

• Monitoring and recordkeeping

These Rules become effective 30 days after a public hearing.

# II. DEFINITIONS

**Accessory Structure:** A structure connected to or located near a home or building, such as a garage, carport, shed, yard structure, fence, or wall.

**Arthropod:** An invertebrate animal with a segmented body and jointed legs (insects, spiders, crustaceans).

**Noxious Arthropods:** Arthropods that harm humans by biting or stinging skin.

**Department:** The San Francisco Department of Public Health.

# IV. RESPONSIBILITIES OF TENANTS

**1\. Housekeeping**

Tenants must keep their homes **reasonably clean and orderly**. This includes storing garbage correctly, keeping food covered, and avoiding clutter that can attract pests. Tenants must not feed wildlife or pigeons.

**2\. Reporting Problems Early**

Tenants must report any signs of pests in their unit or shared areas as soon as possible. DPH and the Rent Board recommend that tenants submit complaints **in writing** and keep a copy for their records.

**3\. Cooperation**

Tenants must cooperate with building managers and pest control companies. This includes allowing reasonable access for inspections and treatments.

**4\. Preparing for Treatment**

Tenants must follow instructions from pest control operators to prepare their unit for treatment.

**5\. Cleaning and Removing Clutter**

Tenants must remove unnecessary items and store belongings in an organized way to make inspections and treatments effective.

**6\. Meetings or Notices**

DPH may issue a Notice of Violation or schedule a meeting with tenants who do not cooperate or who create conditions that allow pests to live or spread. If needed, DPH may declare a unit a **public health nuisance**.

**7\. When Management Doesn’t Respond**

If a landlord or manager does not correct a reported problem, tenants may contact:

**Healthy Housing and Vector Control**

**415‑252‑3800**

Assistance is available in multiple languages. Tenants may also call **311**.

**8\. Temporary Relocation**

If health or safety is at serious risk, the Director of Health may require temporary relocation. Tenants must cooperate with DPH and management during this process.

**9\. Commercial Food Businesses**

Businesses must store and remove food waste properly. Poor garbage management can threaten health and may lead to permit suspension or revocation.

# V. RESPONSIBILITIES OF RENTAL PROPERTY OWNERS AND MANAGERS

**1\. Training**

Owners/managers must train their staff on:

• How to inspect units

• How to handle complaints

Owners/managers must investigate tenant reports of pest activity **within 72 hours**.

Accurate records must be kept for at least **two years** and made available to DPH on request. Records must include:

• All reports of infestations

• All services performed by pest control operators

• Dates inspections occurred

Owners/managers must hire a **licensed Pest Control Operator (PCO)**. Proof of licensing must be provided when requested.

**5\. Garbage Storage and Removal**

Owners/managers must provide enough trash containers and ensure garbage is removed often enough to prevent overflowing. The area must be kept clean and maintained.

**6\. Pest Inspections**

PCOs must perform a complete inspection and determine the type and level of infestation.

**7\. Least‑Toxic Methods**

DPH recommends using the least‑toxic methods first, especially in homes where children or medically vulnerable individuals live.

**8\. Advance Notification to Tenants**

Owners/managers must ensure tenants receive proper written notice before pesticide treatments, following state laws. (See Appendix A.)

**9\. Required Training**

DPH may require owners or managers with repeated violations to complete training on sanitation, pest prevention, and building maintenance.

**10\. Assistance for Tenants Who Cannot Prepare Their Unit**

If a tenant cannot safely prepare their unit due to disability or medical need, owners/managers must contact appropriate support services.

# VI. LICENSED PEST CONTROL OPERATORS (PCOs)

**2\. Full Inspection**

**6\. Inspect Adjacent Units**

**7\. Proper Handling of Pesticides**

PCOs must follow all laws and product label directions when transporting, mixing, applying, or disposing of pesticides.

**8\. Use of Additional Tools**

PCOs may use vacuuming, steam, heat, or freezing methods as appropriate.

PCOs must give instructions on safely removing infested furniture or materials so pests do not spread.

# VII. Vector Prevention

Successful prevention of rodents and other vectors requires long‑term commitment from all responsible parties. Prevention efforts should focus on:

1. Proper sanitation  
2. Proper storage practices  
3. Proper landscape management  
4. Eliminating standing water to prevent mosquito breeding  
5. Routine inspections, monitoring, and recordkeeping

## 1\. Proper Sanitation

Garbage and waste must be stored and handled in a clean and sanitary manner.

* Every dwelling or unit must have at least one rigid plastic or metal container large enough to store all garbage generated inside the unit.  
* Containers must be rodent‑proof, insect‑proof, watertight, and fitted with tight‑closing lids.

Plastic bags may be used inside containers, but not as standalone storage.

* Food spills and utensils must be cleaned daily to avoid attracting pests.

## 2\. Proper Storage Practices

Items such as crates, pallets, lumber, tires, firewood, or scrap metal must be stored in ways that allow monitoring for pests and prevent harborage for insects, rodents, or wildlife.

* Commercial establishments must organize materials to allow easy inspection and pest monitoring.

Tenants must store personal belongings to prevent access to food and reduce clutter that can hide pests.

* Unnecessary items must be discarded or recycled.

When temporary storage of pallets, lumber, tires, or scrap is required, items must be placed so they are easy to inspect and do not create harborage or breeding sites.

* Stored items must be stacked neatly and elevated at least **6 inches** off the ground, with **6 inches of clearance** from exterior walls or fences.  
* Stored items must not collect water. Any item that could hold water must be **drained, covered, or inverted** to prevent mosquito breeding.

## 3\. Landscape Management

When rodent activity is identified, owners shall:

* Maintain tree and shrub branches at least **3 feet away** from walls or roofs to prevent rodents from climbing or jumping onto structures.  
* Install metal or heavy plastic tree‑protective sleeves to prevent rodents from feeding on fruit or citrus trees.  
* Remove dropped fruit regularly, especially citrus, figs, and palm fruit.  
* Thin dense trees, vines, and shrubs that can hide nests or mosquito resting areas.  
* Remove or thin groundcover that can hide burrows or runways.  
* Keep a **24‑inch clear space** along fences or exterior walls to reduce rodent movement.  
* Keep fence tops free of vegetation to allow visibility of rodent activity.  
* Apply gravel around foundations or in areas where rodents may burrow.  
* Remove bird feeders temporarily if rodent activity is observed.

## 4\. Standing Water (Mosquito Prevention)

To prevent mosquitoes that can carry **West Nile virus**, owners and managers must eliminate all standing water.

Standing water attracts mosquitoes, rodents, and other unwanted wildlife.

### West Nile Virus Information (Public Health Guidance)

West Nile virus is carried by infected birds. People can become infected from a mosquito that has previously fed on an infected bird.

For information about West Nile virus, call **415‑252‑3806**, **1‑877‑WNV‑BIRD**, or visit **SFMosquito.org** or **WestNile.ca.gov**.

### Required Precautions:

* Repair or install window screens; close windows before dusk.  
* Cover exposed skin when mosquitoes are active and use repellent as appropriate.  
* Do not over‑water gardens; allow soil to dry between watering.  
* Report unusual mosquito activity to the San Francisco Department of Public Health, Mosquito Control Program: **415‑252‑3806** or **311**.  
* If anyone has been bitten and develops high fever with stiff neck or severe headache, they should contact a healthcare provider immediately.  
* See **Appendix C: Homeowner Checklist** for additional guidance.

## 5\. Routine Annual Inspections

* Rental agreements should authorize owners or managers to conduct **annual inspections** of rental units to identify conditions affecting habitability, including vector infestations, leaks, mold, or other hazards.  
* Annual inspections allow early detection and correction of problems before they cause significant damage.

## 6\. Routine Monitoring and Recordkeeping

Using a simple checklist to survey property helps prevent pest problems.

Monitoring should include:

* Entry points, food sources, water sources, and possible harborage areas  
* Pest abundance to determine whether infestation is increasing  
* Effectiveness of control measures already in use  
* Whether professional assistance is needed  
* Resource planning needs

Recordkeeping provides critical documentation to support future actions and enforcement.

# VIII. Vector Exclusion

Residential and commercial buildings must be constructed and maintained to prevent pests from entering. Effective **vector exclusion** includes, but is not limited to:

* Repairing gaps larger than **¼ inch** around exterior doors and windows.  
* Sealing openings in exterior walls—especially around pipes or conduits—using hardware cloth, copper mesh, caulk, sheet metal, concrete, or mortar.  
* Covering exterior openings to attics or crawl spaces with corrosion‑resistant wire screening, perforated vinyl, or similar materials to prevent entry by birds, bats, rodents, and other animals.  
* Clearing brush and grass at least **2 feet** around building foundations.  
* Trimming shrubs, vines, and trees so branches are at least **3 feet** away from roofs or balconies.  
* In areas with fly or mosquito problems, ensuring all windows, doorways, and ventilation openings have screens of at least **16‑mesh**, or other tight‑fitting devices that effectively prevent entry.

# IX. Signs of Rodent Activity

Rodents are most active at night, so direct sightings are not usually the first sign of a problem. Regular monitoring for signs of rodents is essential.

Evidence of rodent activity may include:

* **Droppings.**

Small droppings (smaller than a grain of rice) are usually from mice.

Soft droppings indicate recent activity.

* **Damaged food containers.**

Bags, boxes, or packages with holes or gnaw marks are signs of rodents.

* **Rub marks and smudges.**

Dark, greasy marks along walls or edges of objects can indicate areas rodents travel frequently.

* **Gnaw marks and holes.**

These may be new or old; both indicate rodent presence.

* **Burrows or nests.**

Burrows and nesting materials should be removed promptly.

* **Footprints or tail marks** in dust, flour, or sand.  
* **Damage to fruits or plants**, such as hollowed lemons.  
* **Noises at night**, such as scratching, chewing, or running.  
* **Pet behavior.**

If pets investigate a specific area repeatedly, check that area for signs of rodents.

Tenants and owners should refer to the safe cleaning procedures in **Section XIII** for guidance on proper cleanup of rodent droppings.

# X. Preventing Pigeon Roosting, Disease, and Property Damage

Pigeon droppings, feathers, and nesting materials can contain mites, lice, allergens, bacteria, fungal spores, and other organisms that may cause illness. Accumulated pigeon waste is a public health nuisance and can damage paint, metal, and building materials.

In San Francisco, it is **illegal** to poison, trap, or shoot pigeons.

**Pigeon-proofing** means preventing pigeons from roosting or nesting on buildings or property. This may include design features, physical barriers, or timed deterrents.

Effective pigeon‑proofing includes:

* Ensuring that exterior doorways, stairs, windowsills, eaves, awnings, roof surfaces, gutters, and light‑wells are constructed or modified to discourage roosting and nesting.  
* Seeking approval from the Fire Department before installing netting or other modifications that could affect building exits, including stairwells.  
* Ensuring that no bird seed, pet food, or food waste is accessible where pigeons are creating a nuisance under Health Code Section 581(b).

Tenants must be notified to stop feeding pigeons.

* Contacting **415‑252‑3805** to obtain signs or flyers advising the public not to feed pigeons.  
* Keeping property free of pigeon waste, especially at entryways, windowsills, stairways, light‑wells, schoolyards, drinking fountains, easements, and ventilation equipment areas.  
* Using plenty of water and disinfectant when removing droppings or nesting materials.

# XI. Trapping for Rodent Control

* Glue traps may be useful for monitoring cockroaches and fleas, but they can pose hazards to children, pets, and wildlife.  
* If glue traps are used, vegetable oil can help remove glue from skin or hair.  
* Snap traps can be effective when used properly for mouse control.  
* For rat control, DPH recommends that trapping be performed by a **licensed Pest Control Operator (PCO)**.  
* Snap traps may be used with or without bait.

If bait is used, choose foods known to attract rodents, such as:

• bacon grease on bread

• raisins

• chocolate candy

* Rodents must press the trigger while taking the bait to activate the trap.

# XII. Snap Traps

* Snap traps are effective when used by experienced individuals.  
* A licensed PCO is the preferred person to perform trapping for rat infestations.  
* Continue to set traps for about **one week** after the last rodent is caught to confirm that activity has stopped.  
* Snap traps can be hazardous to children and pets.  
* Safer trap designs—enclosed traps or box traps—are available online and at hardware stores.

# XIII. When to Call a Licensed Pest Control Operator

Call a licensed PCO when:

* Routes of pest entry are difficult to locate.  
* A significant infestation requires an experienced professional.  
* A building has **more than six units** (larger buildings often need coordinated service).  
* Trapping has not eliminated a mouse infestation.  
* Tenants continue to report pest activity.  
* Rodenticides may be necessary for rat control.  
* Multiple living units are, or are likely to be, affected.  
* Exclusion or deterrent work is needed for roofs, eaves, or balconies.  
* Dangerous vectors are involved (for example, wasps or spiders of public health concern).

# XIV. Procedures for Safe Cleanup

Because of recent cases of hantavirus—a serious respiratory disease spread by certain rodent droppings—the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends using personal protective equipment (PPE), such as disposable gloves, when cleaning up dead rodents, rodent urine, droppings, nesting materials, or other contaminated materials.

These procedures also apply to cleaning up bird waste.

See **Appendix B** for additional guidance on hantavirus.

### Safe Cleanup Steps

* Spray urine stains or feces with an appropriate disinfectant such as a **bleach solution** (sodium hypochlorite) or another approved disinfectant until thoroughly soaked.

With your hand covered by a plastic bag, use a paper towel to pick up the material.

Wrap the paper towel in the plastic bag and discard it outdoors in a sealed container.

* **Never mix bleach with ammonia** or cleaning products containing ammonia; mixing them can create dangerous chlorine gas.  
* Do **not** vacuum or sweep urine, feces, droppings, or contaminated materials unless they have been disinfected. Sweeping may release airborne pathogens.  
* After droppings have been removed, clean and disinfect the area—especially food preparation surfaces and utensils that may have been contaminated.

Even if urine is not visible, it may be present wherever droppings are found.

* Spray dead rodents with disinfectant or chlorine solution to kill mites, fleas, or other organisms.  
* Avoid direct contact with dead animals. If possible, pick them up using a shovel.

### Removing Rodents from Traps

* Wear disposable gloves or place hands in plastic bags before handling traps.  
* Remove the rodent by lifting the trap’s metal bar and letting the animal fall into a plastic bag, or place the entire trap into a plastic bag and seal it.  
* Place the sealed bag inside a second plastic bag and seal again.  
* Dispose of the double-bagged material in a covered garbage can.

### Reusing Traps

* If a trap will be reused, wash it with detergent, then disinfect it with a bleach solution, and rinse thoroughly.

### Handwashing

* After removing gloves, wash hands thoroughly with soap and water.

If soap and water are not available, use an alcohol‑based hand sanitizer.

# APPENDIX C — Homeowner and Resident Checklist of Mosquito Sources

Mosquitoes **must** have standing water to develop.

This checklist helps identify and eliminate common sources of standing water around homes and properties.

## Checklist of Common Mosquito Sources and Recommended Actions

**Fishpond**

• Stock with fish, remove excess vegetation, and maintain proper construction.

**Swimming Pool & Spas**

• Chlorinate and cover tightly when not in use.

• Keep water off the cover.

• Drain or stock with fish if the pool is not maintained.

**Bird Bath**

• Change water at least once a week.

**Containers**

• Empty, invert, dispose of, or cover to prevent mosquito entry.

• Includes buckets, drums, boats, and any outdoor container.

**Basement & Under House**

• Keep the area dry.

• Repair leaks, dripping pipes, and malfunctioning refrigeration or air‑conditioning units.

• Standing water in basements or under homes is a major mosquito source in urban areas.

**Catch Basins & Storm Drains**

• Do not dispose of litter, oil, paint, or garden waste in drains.

• City and County treat catch basins monthly to prevent mosquito breeding.

**Sump**

• Construct so water does not stand inside.

• Screen to prevent mosquito entry.

**Trim and Thin Shrubbery / Avoid Over‑Watering**

• Dense shrubbery provides resting sites for adult mosquitoes.

• Over‑watering keeps soil damp; allow the ground to dry between watering.

**Tires**

• Dispose of properly or cover so they do not collect water.

**Tree Holes**

• Fill, screen, or remove holes that collect water.

**Septic Tank**

• Keep tightly covered.

• Septic tanks can produce very large numbers of mosquitoes.

**Drains and Ditches**

• Remove litter, weeds, and obstructions so water flows freely.

## Mosquito Life Cycle

**Eggs**

• Deposited singly or in clusters on water surfaces or in places that will later fill with water.

• Eggs hatch into larvae.

**Larvae ("wigglers")**

• Move through the water with a characteristic wiggling motion.

• Typically ¼–½ inch long.

• Very active and feed on microorganisms.

• Easily seen at the water surface.

**Pupae ("tumblers")**

• Active but do not feed.

• Seen resting at the water surface.

• Move with a tumbling motion when disturbed.

• Transformation to adults occurs inside the pupa.

• At completion, the pupa case splits and the adult mosquito emerges.
