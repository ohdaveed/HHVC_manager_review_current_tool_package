Rodent Control (Rats and Mice)</strong></p><p>Property owners must construct and maintain buildings to exclude rodents. To comply with San Francisco Health Code Article 2, Section 92, all openings in building foundations, exterior walls, and roofs larger than 1/4 inch must be sealed.</p><p>Approved Rodent-Proof Exclusion Materials (strictly enforceable):</p><ul><li>Hardware Cloth: Minimum 19-gauge galvanized wire mesh with openings no larger than 1/4 inch.</li><li>Copper Mesh or Steel Wool: Must be packed tightly into gaps and backed with structural sealant to prevent rodents from pulling it out.</li><li>Sheet Metal: Minimum 26-gauge galvanized sheet metal.</li><li>Structural Sealants: Concrete, mortar, or cement to seal masonry gaps.</li></ul><p>Sanitation standards require property owners to keep vegetation, branches, and shrubs pruned at least 3 feet away from building walls or rooflines to prevent rodent runways. A clear perimeter of 24 inches must be maintained around the base of all foundations.</p><p><strong>2. Bed Bug Abatement (Article 11A)</strong></p><p>Bed bug management in San Francisco is governed strictly by Health Code Article 11A. It is critical to differentiate Article 11A (bed bug public health nuisances) from California Building Code Chapter 11A (disability accessibility standards). Under Article 11A:</p><ul><li>Pre-Tenancy Disclosure: Owners must provide a written 24-month history of bed bug infestations in the building to prospective tenants before signing a lease.</li><li>72-Hour Response: Owners must investigate any tenant bed bug complaint within 72 hours of receiving written notice.</li><li>PCO Verification: Owners must hire a licensed Pest Control Operator (PCO) to inspect and verify infestations.</li><li>Management Plan: If an infestation is verified, the owner must submit a written pest management plan to DPH within 10 business days.</li><li>Certified Training: SRO hotel managers must complete DPH-approved bed bug control training every 2 years.</li></ul><p>The strict standard for declaring an infestation resolved is the 28-Day Zero-Activity Rule. A unit is not cleared until a licensed PCO conducts a follow-up inspection at least 21 to 28 days after treatment and confirms zero live bed bugs, active eggs, or signs of infestation.</p><p><strong>3. Tropical Rat Mite Bite Dynamics &amp; Treatment Sequence</strong></p><p>Tropical rat mites (Ornithonyssus bacoti) are blood-feeding ectoparasites of rodents. When their host rodent dies or is excluded from a building, mites migrate into living spaces in search of a blood meal, biting human residents. Because mites cannot survive or reproduce indefinitely without a rodent host, treatment must prioritize rodent exclusion. It is critical to explain to residents that spraying pesticides for mites without sealing out rodents will fail to solve the infestation.</p><p>Mandatory Operational Treatment Sequencing:</p><ul><li>1. Identify and Treat: Locate the rodent nest in attics, wall voids, or crawl spaces, and direct a PCO to treat the nesting area for mites first.</li><li>2. Safe Nest Removal: Safely remove nesting material using wet-cleanup methods (spraying with disinfectant or bleach/water solution) to prevent inhaling dust. Wear N95 respirators and gloves.</li><li>3. Exclusion &amp; Trapping: Seal all rodent entry points (gaps &gt; 1/4 inch) and set snap traps. Do not deploy rodenticides until the mite threat is completely neutralized.</li></ul><p><strong>4. Pigeon Dropping Cleanup &amp; Histoplasmosis Risks</strong></p><p>Pigeon (Columba livia) droppings can harbor Histoplasma capsulatum, a fungus that causes histoplasmosis when spores are inhaled. To protect cleanup crews and the public, droppings must be kept wet with a fine water mist or disinfectant before removal to prevent spores from becoming airborne. Dry sweeping or high-pressure power washing of dry droppings is strictly prohibited.</p><p><strong>5. Ground Wasp Referrals and Dual-Track Protocol</strong></p><p>Ground wasps (yellowjackets) represent a seasonal sting hazard. HHVC enforces wasp control under a strict dual-track protocol:</p><ul><li>On Public Property: Wasp nests on public streets, parks, or sidewalks must be referred immediately to the San Francisco Department of Public Works (DPW) or Recreation and Parks for abatement.</li><li>On Private Residential Property: HHVC retains active inspection and enforcement powers. Under Article 11, Section 581, the program can issue an order to the private property owner requiring them to hire a licensed PCO to destroy and seal the ground nest.</li></ul>
