# Health Code Article 11: Regulatory Communication & Code Interpretation Handbook

## 1. Strategic Foundations for Regulatory Clarity

Translating Health Code Article 11 into plain language is a strategic necessity for the San Francisco Department of Public Health (SFDPH). By dismantling dense legalistic prose, we reduce the "internal jargon" barrier that prevents residents from understanding their rights and obligations. Clear communication is the primary driver for voluntary compliance; when legal authorities and safety standards are accessible, responsible parties can take corrective action before enforcement escalates. This clarity transforms the Health Code from a static legal document into a functional tool for community well-being and improves the efficiency of Healthy Housing and Vector Control (HHVC) operations.

Our digital presence follows a "User-Centered" philosophy mandated by the SF.gov platform. Central to this are the "Services First" and "One page goal" principles. "Services First" prioritizes the user's immediate need—such as reporting a hazard or paying a fee—over the department's internal hierarchy. The "One page goal" ensures that all information required to complete a task exists in a single location, preventing users from losing context while navigating through various Topic pages.

### Regulatory Translation Goals

| Technical/Legal Phrasing          | Plain Language Alternatives                       |
| :-------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------ |
| Responsible Party                 | Person responsible for the problem                |
| Nuisance Abatement                | Fixing the health hazard                          |
| Pest Harborage                    | Places where pests live or breed                  |
| Notice of Violation (NOV)         | Official notice of a health code problem          |
| Prohibited Public Health Nuisance | A health or safety hazard that is against the law |
| Order to Vacate                   | An order to move out for safety reasons           |

By adhering to these translation goals, HHVC ensures that regulatory documentation serves as a bridge to compliance rather than a barrier to understanding.

## 2. The Code Interpretation Framework

A standardized interpretation framework is essential for maintaining a professional, consistent tone across all SF.gov content. By using a predictable structure, HHVC provides transparency regarding enforcement while ensuring accessibility for all San Franciscans. This framework explains not just the letter of the law, but the practical impact on the community, building public trust through clarity.

Every regulatory explanation must include a "Code Interpretation Block" using the following standard components:

- **What the code says:** Provide a concise, direct summary of the legal text from Article 11.
- **What this means:** Explain the practical impact on the reader. This must use Grade 8 language or lower to ensure broad comprehension.
- **Common examples:** List 3 to 4 typical scenarios, such as uncollected garbage, mold in a bathroom, or overgrown weeds.
- **What inspectors look for:** Describe observable evidence used for verification, such as signs of rodent activity or visible moisture.
- **Who may be responsible:** Distinguish between "Owner," "Manager," and "Responsible Party" per Section 580. Clarify that a tenant may be a Responsible Party if they created or contributed to the condition.
- **Why HHVC enforces this:** Link the regulation to public health outcomes, such as preventing disease transmission from houseflies or reducing asthma triggers.

This structure ensures that readers are fully prepared to understand the specific nuisance definitions and responsibilities outlined in Article 11.

## 3. Categorizing Public Health Nuisances (Article 11, Sec. 581)

To help residents identify issues quickly, we categorize nuisances by user-task rather than by internal code sequence. This organization helps users find relevant information without scanning unrelated regulations.

### Public Health Nuisance Categories (Sec. 581)

- **Filth and Waste:** Accumulations of garbage, filth, decayed food, and unsanitary debris.
- **Biological Hazards:** Materials contaminated by human or animal excrement, urine, or other biological fluids.
- **Pests and Infestations:**
  - **Pest Harborage:** Pigeons, skunks, raccoons, opossums, and snakes.
  - **Noxious Insects:** Cockroaches, bed bugs, fleas, scabies, lice, and spiders.
  - **Flying Insects:** Houseflies, wasps, and mosquitoes (including "noxious insect harborage" where these insects breed).
- **Structural and Environmental Hazards:**
  - **Moisture:** Visible or demonstrable mold or mildew in building interiors.
  - **Lead:** Hazards in buildings built before 1979 (presumed to contain lead-based paint).
  - **Sanitation:** Buildings or structures found to be unsanitary.
- **Vegetation:** Overgrowth of grass, weeds, and poisonous plants like Poison Oak or Poison Ivy.
- **Industrial/Air Quality:** Discharge of dense smoke, soot, or objectionable fumes (Sec. 590).

### Responsibility Matrix (Sec. 580 Definitions)

| Nuisance Type            | Primary Accountability                                                                                                             |
| :----------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Garbage accumulation** | **Responsible Party:** Can be the Owner, Manager, or Tenant depending on who created, allowed, or failed to correct the condition. |
| **Lead hazards**         | **Owner or Manager:** Typically those with interest, custody, or control of the property.                                          |
| **Pest infestations**    | **Owner or Manager:** Responsible for investigating complaints and recording/abating infestations.                                 |
| **Poison Oak/Ivy**       | **Owner:** Required to remove and destroy poisonous growth on vacant lots or open spaces.                                          |

Identifying these nuisances triggers the administrative and enforcement procedures necessary for public safety.

## 4. Communicating Enforcement Procedures (Sec. 596)

Communication regarding enforcement must set realistic "After-report" expectations. The handbook must define the legal steps HHVC is authorized to take without promising exact timelines, as outcomes depend on inspector findings and the severity of the hazard.

### Administrative Procedures Workflow (Sec. 596)

1.  **Complaint & Inspection:** HHVC verifies a nuisance based on a complaint. Reports should be routed through **311**.
2.  **Notice of Violation (NOV):** If verified, an NOV is served to the Responsible Party. It describes the nuisance and sets a reasonable time for abatement (not to exceed 30 days).
3.  **Order to Vacate:** For extreme hazards (e.g., lead hazards under Sec. 581(b)(10)), the Director may order the premises vacated.
4.  **Director’s Hearing:** If the Responsible Party fails to comply with the NOV, a public hearing is held to consider a Director’s Order to abate.

### Explaining Relocation Benefits (Chapter 72)

When an Order to Vacate is issued for lead hazards, Compliance Officers must explain relocation benefits in plain language:

- **Tenant Eligibility:** Tenants may be eligible for financial assistance if required to move for health reasons.
- **12-Hour Payment Deadline:** The Responsible Party must provide relocation payments to the tenant at least 12 hours before the tenant is required to vacate.

Understanding these steps clarifies the legal and financial stakes of non-compliance.

## 5. The "So What?" of Penalties and Abatements (Sec. 600)

The "Compliance Layer" of our content strategy uses transparent enforcement consequences to encourage proactive resolution. Articulating penalties helps the user understand the risks of inaction.

### Penalty Hierarchy (Sec. 600)

- **Criminal Penalties:** Violations are misdemeanors. Penalties range from a **minimum of $100** up to $1,000, and/or jail time of 10 days to three months.
- **Civil Penalties:** Up to $1,000 per violation, per day.
- **Administrative Penalties:** Up to $1,000 per violation, per day, assessed via the Director’s Hearing process.

### Strategic Compliance Incentives

- **Safe Harbor Clause:** Per Section 600(c)(1), if a Responsible Party corrects the violation within the time specified in the NOV, the City **shall not** award civil or administrative penalties. This incentive applies only if the party has not been cited for the same violation at that property more than twice in the last 12 months.
- **Presumption of Noncompliance:** Under Sec. 600(b)(2), if a party fails to correct a violation by the deadline, they are presumed to be in noncompliance for all subsequent civil proceedings, streamlining legal enforcement.

## 6. Financial Transparency: Fees, Liens, and Collection

Transparent fee communication is vital for property owners. Cost recovery through these fees ensures the sustainability of the Healthy Housing and Vector Control program.

### Fee Schedule (Sec. 609)

The annual "Vector Control and Healthy Housing Inspection Program Fee" applies to residential buildings with 3 or more units.

| Building Type | Unit Count          | Annual Fee |
| :------------ | :------------------ | :--------- |
| **Apartment** | 3 units             | $25        |
| **Apartment** | Over 30 units       | $103       |
| **Hotel**     | Less than 20 units  | $151       |
| **Hotel**     | More than 175 units | $582       |

### Reinspection Fees (Sec. 609.1)

The annual program fee covers the initial inspection and the **first reinspection**. If violations are not corrected, subsequent visits incur hourly charges:

- **Environmental Health Inspector:** $167.00 per hour.
- **Environmental Health Technician:** $150.00 per hour.
- **Late Penalty:** A $40.00 fee applies if reinspection charges are not paid within 30 days.

### Liens and Special Assessments (Sec. 599)

Unpaid fees and abatement costs result in a **Claim of Lien** against the property. Unpaid balances incur an interest rate of **1.5% per month** (compounded monthly). The City may also pursue a **Special Assessment**, allowing for debt collection via property taxes.

## 7. Digital Implementation & Publication Readiness

Adherence to the "Karl CMS" standards is vital for platform consistency and accessibility. Every editor must perform final QA to maintain public trust.

### Quick Checklist for Editors (Appendix H)

- **Page Type:** HHVC program hubs must use the **Topic** content type (as mandated by the July 2026 Platform Audit). Agency pages are restricted to departmental homepages.
- **Component Limits:**
  - **Callouts** must be used for important body notes.
  - **Alerts** (blue top-banners) are **prohibited** on Transaction and Information pages.
  - **Accordions** are **forbidden** on Information pages to ensure critical steps remain visible.
- **Reading Level:** Target Grade 5-6 for service pages; do not exceed Grade 8 for legal/fee content.
- **Routing:** Confirm "311" is the primary path for reports.
- **SEO/Metadata:** The Meta Description must be clear and no more than **110 characters**.
- **Tone:** Use "you" and "we" to speak directly to the reader in an active voice.

### Publication Decision States

- **Ready for Review:** All checklist items and platform constraints are met.
- **Needs Legal Review:** Wording regarding Sec. 596 or Sec. 600 requires verification.
- **Needs Source Review:** Article 11 content must be cross-checked against the latest Code amendments.

The editor’s primary role is to ensure Health Code Article 11 is a transparent, accessible resource that facilitates a healthier San Francisco through clear regulatory communication.

# Understanding 'Services First': The Resident-Centered Revolution in Civic Tech

In the world of government technology, a quiet revolution is taking place. It is a shift from **institutional-centered design**—where websites are organized around a department’s internal hierarchy—to **user-centered design**, known as the "Services First" movement. As a student designer, you will apply these rules to San Francisco’s Environmental Health services, specifically within the Healthy Housing and Vector Control (HHVC) program. The goal is twofold: to provide San Franciscans with exactly what they need and to save critical work for the agency. By enabling clear self-service, we reduce redundant 311 inquiries and phone calls, allowing HHVC staff to focus on complex inspections and public health crises.

Transitioning to this model offers three primary benefits for a resident:

- **Speed of Service:** Residents find the correct form or information immediately, rather than hunting through department bios.
- **Trust in Government:** High-quality, clear digital services signal a government that is competent, reliable, and respectful of a resident's time.
- **Clarity:** By removing bureaucratic jargon, the government becomes accessible to everyone, ensuring the resident understands their path forward within seconds.

To understand why this shift is vital, we must look at the contrast between legacy government sites and the new generation of civic platforms.

## The Great Contrast: Institutions vs. Individuals

Historically, government websites acted as digital brochures for agencies. A classic example is **FBI.gov**, which centers the institution by covering history, organizational structures, and various programs. Even under a "How we can help" header, a user is forced to "pick which group you belong to from a long list" before finding a service. This group-based navigation fails because it requires the user to know how the government categorizes them.

In contrast, the "success story" of **Healthcare.gov** represents the user-centered shift. Although it launched as a "disaster," civic technologists transformed it into a platform built entirely around a single user need: finding and enrolling in health insurance.

### Comparing Design Philosophies

| Feature                 | Institutional-Centered (e.g., FBI.gov)                                       | User-Centered (e.g., Healthcare.gov)                             |
| :---------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Focus**       | Describes the agency, its history, and internal department functions.        | Focused on helping the resident get something they need.         |
| **Navigation Style**    | Group-based: requires users to pick from a long list of internal categories. | Task-based: organizes content around specific user actions.      |
| **Resident Experience** | Requires the user to understand government structure to find help.           | Residents understand the page’s purpose within the first screen. |

This philosophy is codified into a specific design strategy called the **"One Page Goal."**

## The 'One Page Goal' Strategy

The "One Page Goal" dictates that every page must have **one primary user task**. If a resident cannot understand what a page is for within the first screen, the design has failed. Achieving this requires answering five "Universal Content Standards" to ground the student designer's work.

### Universal Content Standards

1.  **Who is this for?**
    - _Designer insight:_ Avoid internal audience labels like "HHVC Program B." Instead, use resident-facing roles like "Tenants," "SRO residents," or "Property Owners."
2.  **What can I do here?**
    - _Designer insight:_ Front-load your titles with a strong verb so the resident knows immediately if they are in the right place to solve their problem.
3.  **What happens next?**
    - _Designer insight:_ Detailing the process (e.g., "An inspector will contact you within 72 hours") builds trust and reduces anxiety about the "black hole" of government.
4.  **Who is responsible?**
    - _Designer insight:_ Transparency about the agency (HHVC) reinforces accountability, helping the user know who is handling their case.
5.  **Where can I get more help?**
    - _Designer insight:_ Providing a direct path to 311 ensures the user isn't left stranded if their specific situation isn't covered on the page.

Achieving this goal requires moving away from internal agency hierarchies and toward an architecture based on tasks.

## Architecture of a Service: Tasks over Departments

In San Francisco’s **Karl CMS**, content is organized by **user tasks**, not department names. For example, instead of a page titled "Vector Control Department," we use "Report rats or mice." As of the July 2026 platform audit, we use four specific page types to support different stages of a resident’s journey:

- **Transaction Page:** Designed for direct action, such as reporting a pest problem or paying a fee. It features a visible "Primary Action" or 311 reporting link.
- **Information Page:** Provides essential education or guidance, such as how to prevent mold. It is the primary tool for "prevent" and "learn" pathways.
- **Step-by-step Page:** A restored content type for HHVC’s multi-step processes. This is critical for guiding users through complex workflows like permitting or the 72-hour tenant-owner notification process.
- **Topic Page:** These have replaced "Agency" pages as the primary hubs. While Agency pages are limited department homepages, Topic pages allow for "theme-gathering" across services (e.g., "Pests and housing problems"), helping users see all their options in one place.

Even a perfect structure fails if the language is a barrier.

## The Language of Accessibility: Plain Language Standards

Plain language is a tool for civic justice. It ensures that residents with dyslexia or those whose first language is not English can access their government.

> ### 📝 Student Cheat Sheet: Rules for Success
>
> - **Target Grade Levels:** Aim for **Grades 5 to 6** for general services. Complex legal, enforcement, or fee content must not exceed **Grade 8**.
> - **Sentence & Paragraph Length:** Keep sentences under **10 words** and paragraphs under **3 sentences**. White space is your best tool for readability.
> - **The "You and We" Approach:** Use "We" for the department and "You" for the resident. Speak directly to the user to make instructions personal.
> - **The "Active Voice" Test:** Use the **Zombie Test** to find passive voice. If you can add "by zombies" to the end of a sentence (e.g., "The permit was issued _by zombies_"), it’s passive. Use "We issued your permit."
> - **Clarity Over Style:** Avoid **contractions** (e.g., use "do not" instead of "don't") because readers often miss them while scanning.
> - **Professionalism:** Avoid **puns, jokes, or idioms**. They do not translate well and can diminish trust in serious public health services.

## The Final Check: Ensuring Publication Readiness

Before any service page reaches the public, it must pass a rigorous filter based on the "Appendix H" publication checklist. This ensures the page is clean, functional, and correctly routed.

### 5-Point Checklist for Launch

1.  **Correct Routing:** Does the page route complaints through 311 as the primary path?
2.  **User-Centered Identity:** Does the page use resident-facing roles (e.g., "SRO Residents") rather than internal program codes?
3.  **Visible Action:** Is the primary task (the 311 link or payment button) visible before any long educational content?
4.  **No Internal Clutter:** Have all internal drafting notes, "named content owners," and file references been removed from the public copy?
5.  **Plain Language Check:** Is the reading level at Grade 6 or below, and have all contractions and jargon been eliminated?

## Conclusion: The Future of Resident-Focused Government

The shift to "Services First" is not merely a design trend; it is a fundamental commitment to equity and accessibility. By centering the resident instead of the institution, we ensure that every San Franciscan, regardless of their background or language, can navigate their city with confidence.

> **Key Takeaway:**
> "Services First" means organizing government around the resident's needs. Success is measured by how quickly a user can complete a task, not by how much they learn about an agency's history.

# Regulatory Roadmap: Navigating San Francisco’s Health Inspection and Nuisance Abatement Process

Public health laws ensure that the environments where we live, work, and play remain safe and sanitary. In San Francisco, we uphold these protections through **Article 11** of the Health Code. As Senior Regulatory Educators, we use this framework to identify hazards and enforce their correction. This roadmap provides a clear path through the legal landscape of public health enforcement.

## 1. Foundations: Defining Roles and the Legal Landscape

Article 11 provides the legal authority to identify and abate nuisances that threaten the community. Understanding the specific legal definitions is the first step in navigating our enforcement process.

### Who's Who: Key Legal Entities

| Legal Term            | Definition in Plain Language                                                | Why it Matters to You                                                                              |
| :-------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Director**          | The Director of Public Health or their authorized representative.           | They hold the ultimate authority to determine if a nuisance exists and issue legal orders.         |
| **Owner**             | Any person holding title, interest, custody, or control of a property.      | They are ultimately liable for the property's condition and all associated enforcement fees.       |
| **Manager**           | The agent responsible for the day-to-day operation of a property.           | They are the primary contact for inspectors and are responsible for coordinating maintenance.      |
| **Responsible Party** | Anyone who creates, allows, contributes to, or fails to correct a nuisance. | **Insight:** This can be an individual, a tenant, or a legal entity like a corporation or trust.   |
| **Prevailing Party**  | The "winner" in a legal or administrative action.                           | **Insight:** This specifically includes the City in any action where we obtain a court injunction. |

**Public Health Insight:** We define a "Responsible Party" broadly because public health hazards are often the result of collective actions. Because the law includes corporations and trusts as "Persons," liability frequently rests with a legal entity rather than a single individual.

_We use these defined roles to assign accountability the moment an inspection identifies a prohibited condition._

## 2. The Checklist: Identifying Public Health Nuisances

San Francisco Health Code Section 581 identifies specific conditions as public nuisances. We categorize these into three logical groups to help property owners and residents prioritize safety:

### Pests & Infestations

- **Noxious Insects:** This includes cockroaches, fleas, scabies, lice, and wasps.
- **Bed Bugs:** These are a high-priority nuisance. Biological data shows bed bug egg mortality is extremely low—approximately 97% hatch successfully—and populations can double every 16 days.
- **Mosquitoes:** We prohibit any harborage that allows mosquitoes to breed, as they are primary vectors for disease.
- **Animal Harborages:** Accumulations that attract or house pigeons, skunks, raccoons, opossums, or snakes.
- **Public Health Insight:** These pests are a priority because they transmit pathogens and cause significant mental and emotional distress. Rapid growth cycles, like those of bed bugs, require immediate intervention.

### Sanitation & Waste

- **Filth & Garbage:** Any accumulation of decayed food, unsanitary debris, or waste material.
- **Biological Hazards:** Any material contaminated by animal or human excrement, urine, or other biological fluids.
- **Spoiled Food:** Any food or drink that is tainted, decayed, or unfit for consumption.
- **Public Health Insight:** We prioritize waste management because it is the most effective way to prevent the spread of bacteria and viruses in densely populated urban areas.

### Structural & Environmental Hazards

- **Mold & Mildew:** Visible or demonstrable growth inside any building or facility.
- **Lead Hazards:** Paint on any structure built before 1979 is presumed to be lead-based and is a nuisance if it creates a hazard for children (up to 72 months of age).
- **Hazardous Growth:** Vacant lots or open spaces infested with **Poison Oak** (_Toxicodendron diversilobum_) or **Poison Ivy** (_Rhus toxicodendron_). Upon notice, the Code requires the immediate **removal and destruction** of these plants.
- **Soot & Smoke:** Furnaces or chimneys that discharge dense smoke or objectionable fumes that deposit particles on surrounding property.
- **Public Health Insight:** These hazards address long-term environmental risks, including respiratory illness, neurological damage in children, and biological exposure.

_Once we identify a nuisance, we initiate a formal legal sequence to ensure the hazard is corrected._

## 3. The Abatement Sequence: From Complaint to Compliance

We enforce a strict timeline to ensure equitable public safety and provide the Responsible Party with a clear window for correction.

### **Step 1: The Complaint**

The process begins when we receive a written or oral complaint. The Director then inspects the premises to verify if a nuisance exists under Section 581.

### **Step 2: Notice of Violation (NOV)**

If we find a nuisance, the Director must serve an NOV to the Responsible Party within 15 days. This notice is a **public record** and identifies the specific problem that must be fixed.

### **Step 3: The Abatement Period**

The Responsible Party is typically given **30 days** to fix the issue. However, the Director has the "discretion" to grant an extension if it is reasonably necessary to ensure the nuisance is properly and safely abated.

### **Step 4: Reinspection**

Our inspectors must return to the property to verify the fix. While the "Vector Control and Healthy Housing Fee" covers the first reinspection for larger buildings, subsequent checks incur hourly fees.

> **High-Severity Intervention: Order to Vacate**
> If we determine that a nuisance is so severe that residents must be moved to protect their health (such as extreme lead hazards), the Director issues an **Order to Vacate**. The property cannot be reoccupied until we provide written permission.

_If the Responsible Party fails to act within the given timeframe, we escalate the matter to a formal administrative hearing._

## 4. Escalation: Director’s Hearings and Official Orders

When a Notice of Violation is ignored, the process shifts from a request for compliance to a formal administrative mandate.

| Feature                | Director’s Hearing                                                   | Director’s Order                                                                                                  |
| :--------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Timing**             | Held within 60 days of the abatement deadline.                       | Issued within 30 days after the hearing concludes.                                                                |
| **Purpose**            | A public forum to present evidence and testimony.                    | A final written decision that dictates the required actions.                                                      |
| **Potential Outcomes** | Determination of nuisance existence; decision to seek attorney fees. | Mandatory abatement deadline; **revocation of Certificate of Sanitation** (for hotels); liability for City costs. |

### Consequences of a Director's Order

A Director's Order carries three primary legal consequences:

1.  **Mandatory Abatements:** The owner is legally required to fix the issue, usually within 30 days.
2.  **Liability for City Costs:** If the City must step in to abate the nuisance, the owner is "indebted" to the City for all costs and fees incurred.
3.  **Potential Liens:** We record unpaid costs against the property title, which can lead to the property being sold to cover the debt.

**Legal Insight:** We may recover "reasonable attorney's fees" from the losing party. However, we must provide a formal notice of our intent to seek these fees at the start of the enforcement action.

_Failure to comply with these orders leads to the highest level of financial and legal penalties._

## 5. The Cost of Non-Compliance: Fees, Penalties, and Liens

We utilize three tiers of penalties to ensure the Health Code is respected and community health is protected:

- **Criminal:** Violating an order to vacate or resisting enforcement is a misdemeanor. Conviction can result in fines up to $1,000 and jail time ranging from 10 days to 3 months.
- **Civil:** The City Attorney may file a civil action for penalties up to **$1,000 per day** for each day a violation continues.
- **Administrative:** As an alternative to court, we can impose administrative fines of up to **$1,000 per day**.

### Fee Structure

| Fee Type                                 | Amount                                       | Applicability                                                                                                                  |
| :--------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Vector Control & Healthy Housing Fee** | Varies by unit count (e.g., $25 for 3 units) | Annual fee for residential buildings with 3+ units. **Exemption:** 3-unit buildings are exempt if the owner occupies one unit. |
| **Reinspection Fee**                     | **$167.00 per hour**                         | Charged for the second and all subsequent checks to verify a fix.                                                              |

**The Lien Process:** If a party fails to pay abatement costs or fees within **45 days**, we file a **Lien** with the County Recorder. This debt includes a significant penalty: **$1,000 or 10% of the costs, whichever is higher**. Additionally, we charge **1.5% monthly interest** (compounded monthly) from the date of recordation. Unpaid assessments may eventually result in the property being sold by the Tax Collector.

_This roadmap ensures that every stakeholder understands the goal of Article 11: clarity, compliance, and the protection of community health._

## 6. Summary & Resources

### "Grok It": Three Key Takeaways

1.  **Responsibility is Broad:** Liability extends to anyone who creates or allows a hazard, including legal entities like corporations.
2.  **Timelines are Firm:** The process moves from an NOV (15 days) to an abatement window (30 days) to a hearing (60 days) to ensure hazards do not linger.
3.  **Non-Compliance is Expensive:** Delaying repairs leads to hourly inspection fees, daily fines of $1,000, and liens with high interest and mandatory penalties.
